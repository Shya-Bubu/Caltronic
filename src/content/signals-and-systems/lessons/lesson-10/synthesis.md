# Stability of Systems - Synthesis

This lesson gives you a compact but powerful chain of reasoning:

1. Start from the transfer function $H(s)$.
2. Find the poles from the denominator.
3. Infer the natural modes that appear in $h(t)$.
4. Decide whether those modes decay, stay bounded, or grow.
5. Use absolute integrability of $h(t)$ to test BIBO stability.

That chain is one of the most useful patterns in the entire course.

## The Core Stability Rules

- If **all poles satisfy** $\operatorname{Re}(p_i) < 0$, the natural response decays and the system is asymptotically stable.
- If poles lie on the **imaginary axis without repetition**, the response can stay bounded but not decay. That is marginal stability.
- If **any pole has positive real part**, or if an imaginary-axis pole is repeated, the response grows without bound and the system is unstable.

Those rules are not arbitrary. They come straight from the time functions generated by inverse Laplace transforms:

- $e^{pt}$ decays only when $\operatorname{Re}(p) < 0$
- $e^{\sigma t}\cos(\omega t + \theta)$ decays only when $\sigma < 0$
- a repeated pole introduces factors like $t e^{pt}$, which can grow even when $p$ is on the imaginary axis

## Why the RLC Example Matters

The series RLC example is a beautiful checkpoint. Its poles are

$$
p_{1,2} = -\frac{R}{2L} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{R}{2L}\right)^2 - \frac{1}{LC}}
$$

or, in the underdamped case,

$$
p_{1,2} = -\frac{R}{2L} \pm j\sqrt{\frac{1}{LC} - \left(\frac{R}{2L}\right)^2}
$$

The real part stays $-\frac{R}{2L}$, so for positive $R$ and $L$ the poles remain in the left half plane. The waveform may change its shape, but the circuit stays stable.

## The Practical Test You Should Remember

For BIBO stability, the clean result is:

$$
\int_{0}^{\infty} |h(t)|\,dt < \infty
$$

If that condition holds, a bounded input cannot produce an unbounded output.

This is the criterion you should carry into filters, control loops, communication channels, and circuit transients. It is the practical engineering version of stability.

## What Comes Next

From here onward, you should be able to look at a transfer function and immediately ask:

- where are the poles?
- what natural modes will appear?
- do they decay?
- is the impulse response absolutely integrable?

That is the right habit. It turns stability from a memorized rule into a fast engineering judgment.
