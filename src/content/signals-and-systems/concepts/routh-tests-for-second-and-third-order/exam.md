# Exam Focus - Second- and Third-Order Routh Tests

## What examiners like to ask

This topic usually appears in one of two forms:

- a direct stability test for a second-order or third-order denominator
- a trap question where all coefficients are positive, but the third-order product condition fails

## Fast method in the exam hall

Write the polynomial in descending powers of $s$ first. Then:

1. if it is second order, check $a_1>0$ and $a_0>0$
2. if it is third order, check $a_2>0$, $a_0>0$, and $a_1 a_2>a_0$
3. write a final sentence that says whether the first column is positive and therefore whether the system is stable

You can rebuild the short Routh array if you want to show working, especially for the third-order case. That often earns cleaner method marks.

## Common mistakes

- assuming positive coefficients automatically mean stability
- forgetting the third-order inequality $a_1 a_2>a_0$
- copying coefficients in the wrong order when building the short array
- giving only inequalities without a final stability conclusion

## What to remember under pressure

For second order, positivity is enough. For third order, positivity is not enough by itself. The extra product condition is the mark-winning step.
